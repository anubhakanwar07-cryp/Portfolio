The problem

Reps going live without ever practising

Emversity's admissions team was scaling. New reps were trained once, in a classroom, and sent into live calls within days. Attrition was high. Objection handling was inconsistent. The knowledge that lived in top performers' heads wasn't transferring.

The only way to get good at sales is repetition — but repetition requires a sparring partner willing to play objecting customer on demand. No such thing existed. Reps practised on real leads, which meant real leads absorbed all the learning curve.

"We keep training them on the same things. The problem is they know what to say. They just can't say it when someone pushes back."

User research

Two types of rep. One platform.

Conversations with the sales team and team leads revealed a clean split in what reps needed. Both needed to improve — but for different reasons and at different points in their learning curve.

User 01 · New Rep

The Newcomer

→Doesn't know the product well enough to improvise
→Needs structured learning before any roleplay — knowledge first, practice second
→Confidence comes from preparation, not instinct
→The quiz gate was built specifically for this person
User 02 · Experienced Rep

The Practitioner

→Knows the product — needs to sharpen objection handling
→Wants live roleplay without wading through basics
→Values the post-session analysis — what specifically failed and why
→Wants manager visibility — their score matters for performance reviews

Paths rejected

Why the standard playbook didn't work

Dropped

More classroom training sessions

Passive learning doesn't transfer to live calls under pressure. Reps know what to say but can't say it when someone pushes back. Adding sessions doesn't close the practice gap — it just adds more content to forget.

Dropped

Static script PDFs + call guides

Tried before at Emversity. Near-zero adoption. Reps don't read PDFs before calls — they need the answer the moment the objection hits. A script is useless at 11pm before a 9am follow-up.

Dropped

Skip quiz, go straight to roleplay

Roleplay without foundational knowledge produces confident bad habits, not good ones. A rep who can sound convincing without knowing the product is a liability, not an asset. The quiz gate was non-negotiable.

The decision

Learn. Practice. Improve. In that order.

Three stages that mirror how skill actually builds. Not a feature list — a sequence. Each stage is gated by the previous one. You cannot roleplay until you pass the knowledge check. You cannot review your performance until you've completed a session. The order is the product.

Option A · Selected

Quiz-gated roleplay

PRO Forces knowledge before practice
PRO Prevents confident bad habits
PRO Newcomers and practitioners both served
CON Friction for experienced reps who want to skip ahead
Option B

Direct roleplay access

PRO No friction — jump straight to practice
CON Newcomers practise without knowing the product
CON Reinforces bad habits under pressure
CON Undermines the core value proposition
Trade-off accepted

The quiz gate frustrated early users who wanted to skip straight to roleplay. I held the line. Practice without knowledge produces confident bad habits, not good ones. The gate was product integrity, not UX friction. Experienced reps clear it in minutes — but it exists for the newcomers who need it.

The work

What I built and how it works

PitchMate is three products in one sequential flow. Video-based knowledge modules with embedded quizzes. An AI roleplay simulator. A structured performance analysis layer. Each one feeds the next.

Training flow — 8-stage curriculum, knowledge stages gated before pitch practice

Every video module pauses at key moments to surface a knowledge check. A mandatory correct answer before the video continues. The quiz gate isn't just at the end — it runs through the entire learning phase, preventing passive watching.

Interactive video — auto-pause at key moments, mandatory correct answer to proceed

Once the knowledge check is cleared, Rhea appears. Rhea is the AI prospect — she plays a student, a budget-conscious parent, or a working professional depending on the scenario. She raises real objections, adapts dynamically to the rep's responses, and doesn't accept surface-level answers. Auto-repetition runs until the rep achieves mastery score.

AI Roleplay Simulator — Rhea plays a live prospect, adapts to responses, throws real objections

After every session, the rep gets a structured performance breakdown — pitch evaluation across five dimensions, specific objection handling analysis, manager comments, and a recommendation for the next session. The analysis is where improvement actually happens.

Post-session analysis — pitch score, objection handling breakdown, manager comments, next session suggestion

Reflection

Three things I'd do differently.

01

Build the manager dashboard earlier

The post-session analysis is built for the rep. The manager dashboard — showing cohort-level performance, which reps are stuck on which objections, who needs coaching — was planned but not shipped. Managers were the economic buyer and I underinvested in their visibility. That was a product strategy mistake, not just a feature gap.

02

Instrument the quiz gate decision

I made the quiz-gate decision on first principles without setting up instrumentation to measure its effect on downstream roleplay quality. The 3× completion rate suggests it was right — but I was measuring activity, not skill development. I should have defined what "better rep" looks like in data before shipping the gate, not after.

03

Allow experienced reps to test out of knowledge stages

The uniform quiz gate created friction for experienced reps who already knew the product cold. A pre-assessment that let them bypass modules they'd already mastered would have improved adoption among the practitioner segment without compromising the gate for newcomers. I knew this during build and deprioritised it for launch speed.
