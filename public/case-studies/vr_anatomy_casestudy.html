01 · My side of the build

The work that happens before a single polygon exists.

A VR build lives or dies on decisions made months before anyone puts on a headset. My job was the paper layer — turning "we should teach anatomy in VR" into something an engineering team could actually build, on a budget leadership could actually approve.

Workstream 01 · Scope

Which simulations ship first — and what kind of thing each one is

Three simulations made the cut: Basic Wound Dressing, Hand Hygiene & PPE Protocol, and Human Anatomy. The critical scoping call: the first two are step-by-step procedures, Anatomy is an open exploration — two fundamentally different interaction models, scoped as such from day one instead of forcing everything into one template.

Workstream 02 · Business requirements

Turning learning goals into buildable specs

The BRD translated "students should understand anatomy spatially" into concrete requirements: 8 toggleable anatomical systems, 3 interaction modes, male and female models, grab-and-examine with snap-back reassembly, and full onboarding — safety instructions and controller mapping — before first entry.

Workstream 03 · Cost analysis

What it costs — and where the leverage is

Costed the build per simulation and weighed the delivery economics: a headset-only experience means every learner needs hardware. The web access path became the cost lever — the same simulations, running in a browser inside the Emverse app, meant the platform could scale to every enrolled student without scaling the headset fleet.

02 · The access decision

Two doors into the same room.

The single most consequential scoping decision: don't gate learning on hardware. Every simulation is reachable through the headset and through the browser — same catalogue, same experience, same anatomy.

Door 1 — HeadsetMeta Quest · full immersion
Door 2 — BrowserEmverse web app · zero hardware
A headset per student doesn't scale. A browser tab does — and the learning shouldn't wait for the hardware budget.

04 · Key decisions

The scoping calls that shaped the product.

Anatomy as open exploration — not another step-by-step procedure.
Wound Dressing and PPE Protocol are sequences with a correct order — procedural sims fit. Anatomy isn't a sequence; it's a space. Scoping it as free exploration with modes, instead of a guided script, matched the interaction model to the learning goal. Trade-off: two interaction frameworks to spec and build, not one
Web access as a first-class citizen, not a demo mode.
The browser path uses the same catalogue, the same simulations, the same interactions — click-and-drag replacing the controller grip. This was the cost-analysis conclusion turned into a requirement: reach scales through software, not hardware procurement. Trade-off: every feature specced twice — controller and mouse
Onboarding inside the first release — safety, positioning, controls.
These are healthcare students, most touching VR for the first time. Safety instructions, headset positioning, and controller mapping were scoped into v1, not deferred — because a nauseous or confused first session is a student who never comes back. Trade-off: more scope in release one, later ship date
Snap-back reassembly — the model always returns to truth.
Students can pull the anatomy apart, but every structure snaps back to its correct anatomical position. Exploration stays safe: no student can leave the model wrong for the next learner, and the reassembly itself reinforces spatial accuracy. Trade-off: constrained freedom — you can't build a "wrong" body
05 · If I ran the next phase

What I'd add before scaling further.

Instrument the learning, not just the logins.

Which structures do students actually isolate? Where does exploration stall? Per-structure interaction analytics would turn the anatomy environment into a curriculum signal — showing educators exactly which systems students avoid or misunderstand.

Measure the outcome claim.

A simple assessment comparison between VR-taught and 2D-taught cohorts on the same anatomy module would give the "improved engagement and retention" promise a real ROI line to point to — the next phase's first deliverable, not an afterthought.

Build the content pipeline before the content backlog.

Three simulations shipped; the roadmap wants more. A repeatable spec-to-ship pipeline — templates for procedural sims, a structure-authoring process for exploratory ones — matters more than any individual fourth simulation.
