The problem

Job searching breaks in three places

The idea came from personal frustration — I was targeting AI PM roles and found the entire process exhausting and disorganised. Most tools help you find a job. None of them help you run a systematic outreach campaign and track it like a pipeline — which is exactly what a serious search requires, especially for roles that never get posted.

01 · VISIBLE MARKET

Finding relevant opportunities

Naukri, LinkedIn, Indeed show you everything or nothing. No system learns what you actually want.

02 · PROCESS

Managing outreach and follow-up

Once you've found something, tracking who you contacted and when to follow up becomes unstructured fast. Opportunities die from poor tracking, not lack of ability.

03 · HIDDEN MARKET

Roles that never get posted

A large share of roles fill through referrals and direct outreach. Most job seekers only ever play in the visible market.

Architecture

Three layers, one closed loop

Mapped out from day one as three independent layers. A rule I held throughout: work on one branch at a time, and don't design the next one while building the current one.

What it does

The three layers, live

Layer 1 — Outreach

Tell it who you are. It finds the recruiter and writes the email.

A 14-field form — background, target role, achievements, tone — feeds both the recruiter search and the email personalisation. When Hunter.io has no data for a company (common for Indian startups), a labelled fallback pool keeps the flow from dead-ending.

Layer 2 — Traction

Every lead lives on a board, not in your memory.

Six columns — Draft, Sent, Follow-up Due, Followed Up, Replied, Cold/Closed. Job search is a pipeline that moves over days and weeks; a Kanban makes the state of it readable in five seconds instead of a spreadsheet you have to actually read.

Problems faced

Three things that broke, for real

BUGHunter.io's database skews heavily US/EU. For Indian startups — CRED, Zepto, Razorpay — it frequently returned zero results, silently killing the app's core value for the companies users actually cared about.
FIXBuilt a mock pool of 20 Indian tech companies with realistic recruiter data as a labelled fallback — never pretending pattern-matched contacts are verified ones. Long-term fix identified but deferred: Apollo.io or the Greenhouse/Lever public ATS APIs, once there's traction to justify the cost.
BUGThe Kanban board's six columns rendered empty on first load, before Supabase data arrived. Technically it was working — just loading. But a blank pipeline reads as "this is broken," not "this is loading."
FIXAdded a loading skeleton — six pulsing placeholder cards while the fetch completes — plus a realistic mock dataset covering every pipeline stage, so the board always looked like a populated, working product even before real data existed.
BUGAfter adding Google OAuth, sessions didn't persist between pages — logged in on Outreach, unauthenticated on Pipeline. The standard Supabase client stores sessions in localStorage, which the server can't read during rendering.
FIXSwapped to @supabase/ssr for cookie-based sessions, readable on both client and server, plus middleware to redirect unauthenticated requests to /login.

What I deliberately left out

Not missing. Decided.

Every item below was considered, discussed with myself, and cut on purpose — the discipline that kept a solo 20-day build from turning into a project that builds forever and ships never.

✕

Bulk outreach

No sending to 50 recruiters at once. Mass outreach is impersonal and risks the relationships this tool exists to build.

✕

Autonomous AI agents

No auto-sending, no follow-ups fired without a click. The user generates, reviews, edits, sends. JSP assists — it doesn't replace judgment.

✕

LinkedIn scraping

Evaluated seriously — best recruiter data lives there. Rejected: LinkedIn actively bans scrapers and sues over it. A product built on a foundation that can vanish overnight is the wrong call.

✕

Analytics, team accounts, pricing

No charts, no shared pipelines, no monetisation in v1. Prove the loop works for one person before building for many.

Honest reflection

The code wasn't the hard part

Twenty days. No prior Next.js or Supabase experience. Three product layers, authentication, and a live deployment.

The technical implementation was AI-assisted — Claude Code, with instruction files I wrote and refined for every branch. That's transparent and intentional: I'm a PM, not an engineer, and using AI for implementation while owning every product and architecture decision was the right division of labour for building this alone.

What mattered was the decision-making — what to build in this version and what to skip, whether to add auth before or after the product worked, whether to scrape LinkedIn or find a legitimate path, how to be honest with users about mock data instead of hiding it behind confident UI. Those are PM decisions. The judgment came from ten months on the job. The code came from twenty days of finally not being afraid of it.

It isn't perfect. Recruiter data is weak for Indian companies. The job feed is limited by its free sources. I named those limits instead of hiding them — in the product, and here.
