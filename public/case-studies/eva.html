The problem

Ready on paper. Not ready for the course.

Emversity was admitting students into professional healthcare programs — BVoc Medical Laboratory Technology, psychology courses, and similar — with no structured way to assess baseline knowledge or aptitude first. Students joined, struggled with the curriculum, and some dropped out mid-course, dragging down completion rates and program reputation.

The existing process was either no screening at all, or informal checks that were inconsistent and easy to game. There was no scalable way to screen hundreds of applicants across multiple programs without hiring a human interviewer for every one of them.

The question: could an AI system screen students the way a human interviewer would — asking the right questions, adapting to the program, and giving admissions a reliable signal on readiness?

2 parts

MCQ test (20 questions, 30 min) + AI interview (20 questions, 30–60 min)

Full proctoring

Camera, microphone, and screen share active throughout both parts

0 humans

The interviewer is fully AI-driven — voice-based, no person on the other side

The product

Two parts. Both proctored, both AI-monitored.

Part 1 is a subject-specific MCQ test mapped to the program applied for. Part 2 is the core of the product — a live, voice-based AI interview that asks scenario-based questions and adapts to the program, evaluating applied thinking rather than recall.

The dashboard — each applicant sees their assigned test, split into its two parts, with time estimates upfront

Getting proctoring right

Four steps. None of them skippable.

Students needed to grant camera, microphone, and screen share access before starting — if any of it failed mid-test, the test's integrity was compromised. The first version had a single "grant permissions" screen, and students kept getting into the test with a broken camera or mic, then raising a support ticket. I redesigned it as four sequential steps, each one verified and shown back to the student before they could move to the next.

1

Instructions

Rules stated plainly — camera monitoring active, tab switching ends the test, face must stay visible. A red warning reinforces the stakes before the student can proceed.

The MCQ experience

Watched, without feeling hunted.

Inside the test, the layout is deliberate: a question navigator on the left, one question at a time in the centre, and the student's own camera feed — visibly labelled "Monitoring" — always present on the right. Seeing your own feed with that badge reduces the temptation to look away or reach for a second device, without needing any aggressive intervention.

The AI interview

No human on the other side.

This is the centrepiece. The AI speaks with a live audio waveform on the left; the student's camera sits on the right. No person is listening on the other end — the AI asks scenario-based questions calibrated to the program, evaluates the response, and moves on. For BVoc Medical Laboratory Technology, that means clinical, practical scenarios — not textbook recall.

Key decisions

Four calls that shaped the product

Four steps, not one screen

Instead of: a single "grant permissions" screen

The single-screen version let students start with a broken camera or mic and then raise a support ticket. Verifying each permission sequentially — and showing it back to the student — caught failures before the test began instead of after. Support tickets on proctoring failures dropped noticeably.

Voice interview, not typed

Instead of: a typed Q&A format

A typed interview simulates a written test, not a real conversation. The point of Part 2 was to assess how someone thinks and communicates under realistic conditions — harder to build, but it's the only version that actually tests what the product claims to test.

Show the student their own camera feed

Small decision, real effect

Students who see their own feed with a "Monitoring" badge are less likely to look away — not because they're scared, but because the visual reminder keeps them anchored. Behavioural design, not surveillance theatre.

Scenario questions, not factual recall

"What should be checked before using reagents?" vs. "A patient's sample shows unexpected results — walk me through your diagnosis"

EVA is trying to predict course performance, not whether someone read the textbook. The interview question bank was pushed toward applied thinking specifically because that's the signal admissions actually needs.

What I'd do differently

Three things I'd build earlier

01

Build the analytics layer from day one

EVA generates interview responses and MCQ results, but the admissions team was reading them manually in the early version. A structured dashboard — score distributions, where students drop off in the setup flow, which MCQ questions fail most often — would have made every cohort's screening smarter than the last, instead of starting from scratch each time.

02

Calibrate interview difficulty by cohort

Early on, the question bank was one-size-fits-all — students from different backgrounds got identical questions regardless of stated experience. I'd build difficulty tiers and let the AI adapt based on early answers, so someone who aces the first two questions gets progressively harder follow-ups instead of staying flat.

03

Set up a formal AI-evaluation loop from day one

I'd compare AI interview scores against admissions-team overrides systematically from the start, instead of relying on directional signal after the fact.
